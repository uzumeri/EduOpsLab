# Teaching as Co-Production

## The Wrong Model: Product Delivery

In product delivery, the producer controls the process. Quality is a function of producer skill and process design. The customer receives the output passively. If the product is defective, the fault lies with the production system.

A lecture is designed as product delivery. The professor prepares content, delivers it, and hands it over. Students are treated as passive recipients. Assessment measures whether the delivered product was received intact. When students fail, the implicit model is that delivery succeeded but reception failed — the product was fine, the customer was defective.

This is not just pedagogically wrong. It's categorically wrong about what kind of process teaching is.

## The Right Model: Co-Production

In service co-production, the customer is a necessary input to the production process. You cannot produce the service without their active participation. The canonical examples from operations — healthcare, therapy, consulting — all share this property.

Education is a near-perfect example. You cannot educate an unwilling or disengaged student any more than you can cut the hair of someone who won't sit still. The student's attention, effort, prior knowledge, honest confusion, and willingness to be wrong are all **direct inputs** to the production process.

## Operational Consequences

If teaching is co-production, then:

- **Student engagement** has the same operational status as teacher preparation — it's a factor input, not a nice-to-have
- **Dropout from engagement** is a process failure, not a customer failure
- **Designing the process to elicit student input** is as important as designing the content
- **Quality measurement** means measuring the joint output, not evaluating the teacher's delivery
- **Student preparation** is incoming material quality control — a student who hasn't done the reading arrives at the production line without necessary inputs

## The Evaluation Absurdity

University teaching evaluation is aimed at the wrong variable. Student satisfaction surveys measure the customer experience of receiving a product — was the delivery pleasant, was the professor clear. They never measure whether the student was an effective co-producer — whether they came prepared, engaged honestly, did the cognitive work.

Teacher evaluation mirrors this: Was the lecture well-organized? Was the professor knowledgeable? These are product delivery metrics applied to a co-production process.

## The Confusion Question as Co-Production Design

"What in this reading confused you?" is a co-production instrument:

- It **recruits students as active participants** in the diagnostic process
- Student confusion reports are a **factor input** that makes the teacher's next session better
- It **forces cognitive pre-processing** — the quality control step at the input stage
- It **reframes confusion as achievement** rather than failure — evidence of engagement deep enough to find the hard parts

## Implications for AI

AI designed under the delivery model optimizes content transmission.

AI designed under the co-production model asks:

1. **Managing input quality** — ensuring students arrive at the co-production moment prepared
2. **Reducing co-production friction** — helping students articulate confusion they can't express
3. **Recycling output as input** — closing the feedback loop between student state and teacher adaptation
4. **Monitoring co-production quality** — not student performance, but whether the joint process is actually running

The bottleneck isn't delivery quality. It was never delivery quality.
