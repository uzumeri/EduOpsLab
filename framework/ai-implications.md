# Where AI Belongs in Education

## The Current Framing Is Wrong

The dominant narrative positions AI as a better content delivery system — smarter lectures, more responsive tutoring, personalized explanations. This operates inside the product delivery mental model. The AI is a better version of the professor-as-transmitter.

That's why so much EdTech underperforms. You can have the world's best AI tutor and still fail if the student isn't functioning as an effective co-producer. The bottleneck isn't delivery quality.

## The Co-Production Frame

If teaching is co-production, AI's highest value is not on the delivery side. It's in:

### 1. Managing Input Quality
Ensuring students arrive at the co-production moment prepared, having done the cognitive pre-processing the process requires. Not just checking whether students did the reading but whether they've reached the threshold of engagement where co-production can actually happen.

### 2. Reducing Co-Production Friction
The biggest losses happen when inputs are present but the joint process breaks down — the student is confused in a way they can't articulate, or embarrassed to reveal, or stuck in a way that derails the session. AI can reduce that friction without replacing the human interaction.

### 3. Recycling Output as Input
Student cognitive output — reported confusions, questions, misconceptions — becomes teacher input for the next session. AI closes that loop faster, more granularly, and across more dimensions.

### 4. Monitoring Co-Production Quality in Real Time
Not student performance in the product-delivery sense, but whether the joint process is actually running — whether both parties are contributing what the process requires at each stage.

## The Bottleneck Reanalysis

Under product delivery, the bottleneck is teacher time — how much content can one person deliver. Current AI tries to solve this by cloning the teacher.

Under co-production, the bottleneck analysis is different. Sometimes the constraint is teacher time. Sometimes it's student preparation quality, or willingness to reveal confusion, or feedback loop speed. Those are different bottlenecks requiring different interventions.

## The Assessment Revolution

Assessment under product delivery measures what was received — did the student absorb the content. Tests, exams, problem sets.

Assessment under co-production measures how effectively the student is functioning as a co-producer. Are they bringing quality inputs? Are they honest about what they don't know? Are they getting better at being educated as a general capability?

AI is well-positioned for this — it requires continuous process monitoring rather than episodic output measurement.

## The Layered Architecture

The generic/content-specific separation suggests:

1. **Generic allocation engine** (top) — scheduling, equity mathematics, content-agnostic
2. **Content-specific AI tutoring layer** (bottom) — determines what AI can handle, generates need signals
3. **Translation layer** (between) — maps content-specific student states into generic priority signals

The generic engine is reusable across subjects. Plug in a new content module and it exposes the same interface to the allocation engine. This separation lets each layer advance independently.

## The Production Function Argument

The most powerful argument for AI in education is not "AI tutors students." It's that AI changes the **production function of teaching** — the relationship between inputs and outputs — in a way that unlocks methods previously ruled out by arithmetic.

If AI handles 80% of routine interactions, the teacher's constrained time can target the 20% where human presence has the highest marginal value — emotionally charged moments, conceptual breakthroughs, neurodivergent students masking distress.
