# Voice & Style Rules

Running list of editorial principles for the EduOpsLab writing (Substack articles and ebook). Add to this as they emerge.

---

## 1. Feeling First, Phrase Second, Term Third

Every concept gets introduced as a **lived experience** first, a **recognizable phrase** second, and a **technical term** third.

**Wrong order:**
> "You're not bad at math. You were batch-scheduled."

"Batch-scheduled" is engineering jargon. It lands in the textbook, not the body. The reader hasn't felt the thing it describes yet.

**Right order:**
> "You're not bad at math. You just heard *'everybody turn to page 47'* on the day you were still stuck on page 31."

The reader hears a teacher they remember. The engineering diagnosis comes later — after they've already nodded.

**The principle:** The reader should recognize the problem from their own classroom memories before you give it a technical name.

---

## 2. Use the Commands

The three-word commands that define batch education are more persuasive than any Theory of Constraints diagram:

- *"Hurry up."*
- *"Keep up."*
- *"You're falling behind."*
- *"Everybody open your workbooks."*
- *"Pencils down."*

These are more powerful than analysis because the reader has heard them in their own head for twenty years. Use them. They are the evidence.

---

## 3. This Is Not a School Story

The argument is about **human learning**, not school administration. The K-12 classroom is where the problem is most visible, but the principle applies everywhere: university, trades, professional development, self-directed learning.

Use examples from outside the school frame to prove the argument is universal:

- **Jo Mackiewicz** — Iowa State professor who became a part-time welder at Howe's Welding and Fabrication. Posts daily on LinkedIn about what she learned, with photos. Over a hundred posts. Pure joy. She doesn't need the money or the prestige. She's experiencing flow learning in an apprenticeship — and documenting it publicly.
- **Apprenticeships generally** — one master, one apprentice. Challenge matched to ability. The bead tells you if you did it right, no test in three weeks. Progress is yesterday's weld vs. today's weld. This is humanity's oldest teaching model and it's pure flow.
- **Hobby learning** — guitar, cooking, woodworking. Nobody batches these. People learn in flow because no institution is forcing a schedule.

The strongest version of the argument isn't "schools should be better." It's "**humans learn in flow by default — school is the place where we break that.**"

---

## 4. The Apprenticeship Is the Control Group

The apprenticeship model is the natural experiment that proves flow works:

- One-on-one or very small group
- Challenge scaled to the learner's current level
- Immediate, tangible feedback (the thing works or it doesn't)
- Co-production is mandatory — you can't weld/cook/build for someone
- The master adjusts to the apprentice, not the other way around
- No batching — you move when you're ready

When someone asks "does flow teaching actually work?", don't cite a study. Point to every apprenticeship, every master class, every residency, every flight lesson that has ever produced mastery. Then ask why classrooms are organized differently.

The homeschooling argument is a subset of this: the parent is an accidental apprenticeship master. Less trained, but operating in a model that doesn't destroy the conditions for learning.
